CANCER PATIENT SUPPORT AND RESOURCES
======================================
Document ID: DOC-004
Last Updated: January 2024
Category: Patient Support

1. EMOTIONAL AND PSYCHOLOGICAL SUPPORT
A cancer diagnosis can bring a wide range of emotions including fear, anxiety, anger, sadness, and feelings of isolation. It is important for patients and their families to know that emotional support is available:
- Oncology social workers can help patients cope with diagnosis and treatment challenges.
- Support groups (in-person and online) connect patients with others going through similar experiences.
- Individual counseling with psychologists or psychiatrists specializing in oncology can help manage anxiety, depression, and adjustment disorders.
- Mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) programs have been shown to improve quality of life in cancer patients.
- Art therapy and music therapy can provide emotional expression and stress relief.
Patients should not hesitate to discuss their emotional well-being with their healthcare team. Mental health is an essential part of cancer care.

2. FINANCIAL ASSISTANCE AND INSURANCE
Cancer treatment can be financially challenging. Resources available include:
- Hospital financial counselors can help navigate insurance and payment options.
- Pharmaceutical company patient assistance programs provide medications at reduced cost or free.
- Organizations like the American Cancer Society, CancerCare, and Patient Access Network (PAN) Foundation offer financial grants.
- Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI) may be available for patients unable to work during treatment.
- The Family and Medical Leave Act (FMLA) provides job-protected leave for medical treatment.
- Many hospitals offer charity care programs for uninsured or underinsured patients.
Patients should inquire about financial assistance as early as possible in their treatment journey.

3. NUTRITION DURING CANCER TREATMENT
Proper nutrition is essential during cancer treatment to maintain strength and support recovery:
- Eat small, frequent meals if appetite is reduced.
- Stay hydrated by drinking plenty of fluids (aim for 8 glasses of water daily).
- Choose high-protein foods to help repair tissue and maintain immune function (eggs, lean meats, beans, nuts).
- Manage nausea by eating bland foods like crackers, toast, and rice. Ginger tea may also help.
- Avoid raw or undercooked foods during chemotherapy when immune system is compromised.
- Consult with a registered dietitian who specializes in oncology nutrition for personalized meal plans.
- Supplements should only be taken under the guidance of the healthcare team, as some may interfere with treatment.

4. EXERCISE AND PHYSICAL ACTIVITY
Regular physical activity during and after cancer treatment has been shown to:
- Reduce fatigue and improve energy levels.
- Improve mood and reduce anxiety and depression.
- Maintain muscle mass and physical function.
- Improve sleep quality.
- Reduce the risk of cancer recurrence.
Guidelines recommend:
- Start slowly and gradually increase activity.
- Aim for 150 minutes of moderate activity per week when possible.
- Include strength training exercises 2-3 times per week.
- Listen to your body and rest when needed.
- Consult with your oncology team before starting any exercise program.
- Cancer rehabilitation programs can provide supervised exercise plans.

5. MANAGING TREATMENT SIDE EFFECTS
Common side effects and management strategies:
- Fatigue: Pace activities, take short naps, prioritize tasks, stay mildly active.
- Nausea/Vomiting: Take anti-nausea medications as prescribed, eat small meals, avoid strong odors.
- Pain: Report all pain to your healthcare team. Pain management may include medications, physical therapy, acupuncture, or nerve blocks.
- Hair Loss: Consider wigs, scarves, or hats. Many insurance plans cover wigs (cranial prostheses). Hair typically regrows 3-6 months after treatment ends.
- Mouth Sores: Use soft toothbrush, rinse with salt/baking soda solution, avoid spicy/acidic foods.
- Neuropathy: Report tingling or numbness. Physical therapy and certain medications may help.
- Lymphedema: Compression garments and specialized physical therapy can manage swelling.

6. SURVIVORSHIP AND FOLLOW-UP CARE
After completing cancer treatment, ongoing follow-up care is essential:
- Follow your survivorship care plan, which outlines recommended follow-up tests and schedules.
- Regular follow-up visits typically occur every 3-6 months for the first 2-3 years, then annually.
- Monitor for signs of recurrence and report any new symptoms promptly.
- Continue cancer screening for other cancers as recommended.
- Address long-term side effects of treatment with your healthcare team.
- Maintain healthy lifestyle habits including regular exercise, balanced diet, and stress management.
- Consider joining a survivorship support group.

7. CAREGIVER SUPPORT
Caregivers play a vital role in cancer care but also need support:
- Caregiver support groups provide emotional support and practical advice.
- Respite care services allow caregivers to take breaks.
- Counseling services are available for caregiver burnout and stress.
- Online resources and educational materials help caregivers understand treatment and care needs.
- Communication with the healthcare team is important to ensure coordinated care.
